The selective shunt, more commonly known as the Warren shunt, was described in the late 1960s. It is effectively a distal splenorenal shunt which results in effective variceal decompression and preservation of hepatic portal perfusion. Several controlled trials have compared the Warren shunt with a variety of non-selective shunts. All of these demonstrated no difference in survival between groups but a reduction in post-operative hepatic encephalopathy in those undergoing selective shunts [19, 20].
