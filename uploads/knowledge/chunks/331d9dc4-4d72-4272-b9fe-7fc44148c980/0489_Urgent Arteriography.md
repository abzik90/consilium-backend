Once a bleeding vessel or AVM is identified through angiography, there are different options for treatment (Fig. 3). Traditionally, embolization was used in upper GI bleeding sources but avoided in lower GI bleeds because of the high rate of bowel infarction. However, over the past 10-15 years, smaller catheter and interventional platforms have become available (from 0.035 ′′ systems to 0.018 ′′ and 0.014 ′′ ). With the newer technology and increasing skill with the technology, embolization has proven safe and is preferred over the previous recommended therapy of vasopressin infusion for LGIB [13]. Vasopressin infusion is effective in stopping a LGIB (5990%), but requires long-term catheter placement, and potential complications from having a catheter in place in the femoral artery for 1-2 days. Embolization is also successful (73-100%) in controlling hemorrhage from a lower GI source, but is technically slightly more challenging and difficult in cases of tortuous vessels or arterial spasms (Fig. 4).
