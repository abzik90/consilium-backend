heading: "Management of Acute Bleeding"
---

Endoscopic sclerotherapy has been an accepted means of treatment for variceal bleeding since the 1930s. The goal of treatment is to control bleeding by thrombosing the veins or thickening the mucosa overlying the veins by injecting sclerosing agents into the varices of the distal (third/two-thirds) of the esophagus. The most commonly used agents are sodium morrhuate, sodium tetradecyl sulfate, and ethanolamine oleate. The sclerosing agent is usually injected intra-variceally, beginning at the gastroesophageal junction and proceeding circumferentially. If active bleeding is identified on endoscopy, the sclerosing agent can be injected inferiorly to the site of bleeding. Care should be taken to avoid injection of agent in the proximal to mid-esophagus. Sclerosant can accidentally be introduced into the azygous vein, ultimately into the pulmonary circulation, causing unwanted complications [13]. Endoscopic sclerotherapy is effective in controlling active bleeding in approximately 80-90% of patients [14]. However, a Cochrane review demonstrated no difference between medical management of bleeding esophageal varices and sclerotherapy [15].
