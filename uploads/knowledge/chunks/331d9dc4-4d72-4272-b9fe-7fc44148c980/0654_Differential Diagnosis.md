heading: "Differential Diagnosis"
---

In general, any GI lesion from the mouth to the anus may cause occult GI bleeding. It is important to recognize that lesions in the upper GI tract have the potential to cause occult GI bleeding. These lesions may include epistaxis, bleeding gums, esophagitis, peptic ulcers, esophageal and gastric malignancies, hemobilia, and angiodysplasias to name a few. Traditional colonic sources of occult blood loss include large colon polyps, colon adenocarcinoma, inflammatory bowel disease, ischemic bowel, hemorrhoids, and anal fissures.
