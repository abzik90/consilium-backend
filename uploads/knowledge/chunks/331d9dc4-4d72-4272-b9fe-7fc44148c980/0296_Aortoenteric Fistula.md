In a patient that is hemodynamically stable, the classic diagnostic study is an upper endoscopy (EGD). EGD should be carried out to the fourth portion of the duodenum. Active bleeding into the duodenum may be visualized. If not, the fistula may be detected by the presence of clot, ragged mucosa, purulent material inside the bowel, or exposed graft material. If no evidence of fistula is seen, EGD may be useful in searching for other sources of bleeding; however, it lacks the sensitivity or specificity to conclusively rule out ADF. More recent reviews in the literature have advocated for initial screening with a CT scan of the abdomen including arterial and portal phase intravenous contrast. CT is less invasive and carries less of a risk of dislodging a thrombus than EGD [9, 12].
