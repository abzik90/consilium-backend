In 1987, Koval [2] reported the impact of applying more aggressive angiographic techniques. In 63 consecutive patients referred for angiography, multiple factors were retrospectively reviewed. Incorporation of pharmacologic augmentation of mesenteric angiography in the setting of lower GI bleeding improved the diagnostic yield from 32% (12/37) to 65% (17/26) with the use of heparin, tolazoline, and streptokinase. Streptokinase was used in two patients and a positive result was found in one of the two. Of the ten patients given agents, eight had a lesion identified angiographically. Another factor predictive of positive angiography was the requirement of transfusion of ≥ 3 units of packed red blood cells in the 48 h prior to angiography. This correlated with a positive study in 66% of cases compared with 17%, in those requiring <3 units of packed red blood cell transfusion.
