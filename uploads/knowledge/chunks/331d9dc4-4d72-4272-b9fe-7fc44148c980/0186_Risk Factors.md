Reports suggest that Dieulafoy's lesions account for only a small percentage of cases of acute upper gastrointestinal bleeding. Several retrospective studies have demonstrated an incidence between 1 and 6% [4-6]. Incidence at other locations is more difficult to estimate due to their infrequency. It has been postulated, however, that the lesion is underappreciated due to the difficulty of visualizing the lesion during endoscopy, especially when it is not bleeding [5, 7, 10]. Case series suggest that the great majority of Dieulafoy's lesions are located within 6 cm of the gastroesophageal junction, most often on the lesser curvature [4, 11]. Dieulafoy's lesions are also often found in the duodenum, and lesions in the small bowel, colon, esophagus, and even the bronchial tree have been reported [12, 13, 14].
