heading: "Malignant Small Bowel Tumors"
---

Metastatic tumors to the small bowel. The small intestine is more frequently involved in secondary and metastatic tumor involvement than as the site of primary neoplastic disease. Tumors may spread to the small intestines by direct extension, by intraperitoneal seeding (colon, ovary, uterus, and stomach neoplasms), or by hematogenous metastasis (melanoma, lung, breast, and renal cell carcinoma) [6]. These metastases have the potential to cause occult or overt bleeding from the small bowel, and secondary involvement of the small bowel should be considered in patients with history of malignancy presenting with GI bleeding. Depending on the extent of disease and degree of bleeding, endoscopic therapy or segmental resection may be necessary to alleviate symptoms.
